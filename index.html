Cope and Evan's Family Papers

The Collection

The Cope Evans Family Papers contain the letters of the closely related Quaker families of Cope and Evans who lived in Germantown, Pennsylvania. The letters in the collection were written between 1732-1911. They discuss family, friends and home life, and reflect the social environment of these Quaker families. Other topics to be found within the letters include Philadelphia history, Haverford history , travels in Europe, education, illness and death, and discussions of national events such as the Civil War, Reconstruction, and the assassinations of Presidents Lincoln and McKinley. They are a rich source of information on a large number of topics (Haverford Special Collections).

The Industrial Age

The Industrial Age was a time of cultural expansion and diffusion through intensifying interconnections, which were not only aided by developments in new systems of transit, but also a result of new values placed on travel. The tectonic nature of such a period so exceptionally different from its predecessor is likely to produce a historic revolution.

Quakers in the Industrial Age

During that unique moment, outward pressures were heavily imposed on the Quaker religion to secularize and culturally assimilate to the increasingly heterogeneous culture of this period. The anachronistic lifestyle and quietism of traditional Quakers directly opposed the modern lifestyles of liberal Quakers, and in effect forced a series of revolutionary separations. These rigid divisions, first seen in the 1827 Separation of Orthodox and Hicksite, and then again in the splintering of the Orthodox faction into the Wilburite and Gurneyites sects, were motivated by a desire to define the Quaker religion to a corresponding lifestyle that mirrored an interpretation of how Quakers ought to participate with God, themselves, and the world around them.
